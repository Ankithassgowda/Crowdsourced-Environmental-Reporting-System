ABSTRACT
Environmental challenges are growing more severe with each passing day, and people
need better ways to report to the authorities. This project creates a An intelligent webbased system that supports citizens easily report environmental issues like illegal tree
cutting, garbage dumping, and water pollution using artificial intelligence. The
application has three main parts. First, regular users can create accounts, take pictures
of environmental problems, and submit reports with details about the location and
what they observed. Second, government administrators have a special dashboard
where they can access and review all the grievances, change their status from pending
to resolved, and keep track of different types of environmental issues. Third, The
intelligent system employs deep learning techniques to analyze the uploaded images
and predict whether they show tree cutting, garbage problems, or polluted water. The
AI model was trained using a dataset of 459 images divided into three categories:
cutting trees (172 images), garbage problems (176 images), and polluted water (111
images). The system uses a special type of neural network called EfficientNet-B0,
which is known for being both accurate and fast. After training the model for 23
rounds, it achieved an accuracy of 91.08%, meaning it correctly identifies
environmental problems in 9 out of 10 cases. The project illustrates how artificial
intelligence can provide support and solve real-world environmental issues by
simplifying the process for citizens to report issues and for authorities to respond
quickly. The system could be expanded in the future to include more types of
environmental problems and even predict where issues might occur before they
become serious. The combination of user-friendly design, accurate AI predictions,
and efficient complaint management makes this system a valuable tool for protecting
the environment and improving communication between citizens and government
agencies.

manual reporting procedures carried out by concerned individuals or planned
government inspections undertaken by environmental protection departments, yet
such traditional techniques remain tediously slow and too often fail to capture large

resources, geographical limitations, and timing factors that allow inadequate
coverage of all areas subject to monitoring. Recent scholarly research studies have

2.1 EXISTING AND PROPOSED SYSTEM
2.1.1 EXISTING SYSTEM
Currently, the systems used to monitor environmental problems largely depend on
people reporting things manually and on government agencies doing physical
inspections. Residents usually need to call special telephones, referred to as
hotlines, or visit government buildings in person if they wish to formally complain
about such a thing as dumping garbage illegally or contaminated water. This oldfashioned method, though somewhat effective, Often delays the reporting
procedure and may be a hindrance for individuals wishing to donate their time to
assist.
Some cities have attempted to simplify things by implementing mobile apps where
individuals are able to report issues instantly using their phones. Yet, even with
such apps, a human administrator nearly always must go and verify whether the
reported problem exists before action can be taken. Most of these systems also
employ extremely simple web-based forms that do not incorporate any automatic
mechanism to verify that the information input is correct or that the problem does
exist. Although some more sophisticated systems may employ basic image
processing, they tend to target only a single particular problem, such as air quality
monitoring or detecting litter, rather than being able to detect any kind of problem.
The majority of these traditional methods of work require human experts to review
each and every one of the incoming reports. The reliance on human experts
reviewing all such complaints eats up a lot of time and consumes plenty of precious
resources which can be better utilized. Government authorities tend to send teams
out to actively patrol grounds and search for environmental offenses, but this
approach cannot possibly survey extremely wide areas optimally, so a lot of issues
may be overlooked. Although devices such as drones or satellite imagery can
efficiently cover large extents for observation purposes, they tend to be rather costly
to purchase and maintain. And in addition, the individuals who use them must be
specially trained, further increasing their cost and level of complexity.
One major problem with existing complaint management systems is that they are
usually not directly linked to the true verification process. This absence of
connection may result in slow responses because data has to be transferred from
one team to another manually. Moreover, the majority of these systems also fail to
give immediate feedback to the complainants, leading to frustration and deterring
people from complaining in the future. Perhaps the most important challenge with
the systems we have now is that they cannot automatically figure out if a reported
environmental issue is truly happening or if it's a false alarm. This inability to
automatically verify reports can lead to officials wasting time and effort on reports
that aren't real, taking away valuable resources which ought to be applied to fix
actual environmental problems.

2.3 TOOLS & TECHNOLOGIES USED
Creating our environmental complaint management system required us to carefully
select and combine several modern software tools and technologies that integrate
effectively to build a platform that is both efficient for processing complaints and
easy for users to navigate. The backend of this system is constructed based on Flask
which is a web framework written in Python and used by developers whenever they
want a tool that is both light and very flexible. Flask made it a lot simpler for our
development team to develop web applications rapidly because It comes with builtin support. for handling the interaction between web browsers and our server,
maintaining user session state whenever individuals log in, and integrating easily
with our database systems.
We selected MongoDB as our main database management system since it is a
member of a class known as NoSQL databases, which offers us far greater
flexibility whenever we have to save various kinds of intricate information such as
digital photographs, in-depth user profiles, and complaint histories that could have
varying amounts of data. The reason MongoDB saves information in documents
rather than standard database tables is that it is perfect for our environmental
grievance system since every grievance will have different amounts of photos,
different types of location data, or additional files attached to it, and MongoDB can
save all of them without any complications.
For us to make sure our system is safe and only the Only permitted individuals can
reach confidential information. administrative functions, we added a specialized
application called Flask-Login, which manages user authentication and sessions.
Using this application, we are guaranteed when people log in to our system, their
login information is safe and that access to sections of the system displaying and
managing citizens’ grievances is restricted solely to administrators.
The AI aspect that automatically scans and classifies the images users provide with
their complaints runs on PyTorch, which is arguably the strongest and cutting-edge
deep learning frameworks used today. PyTorch provided our coding team with the
capability to develop and train our image recognition model successfully, and we
actually utilized something referred to as EfficientNet-B0 architecture because it
provides very high accuracy in identifying various kinds of environmental issues
yet can also be used on computers which do not have very expensive hardware or
huge amounts of processing power to operate.
When individuals post pictures with their environmental grievances, our system has
to process the images first before the artificial intelligence model is able to read
them correctly. We achieved this image processing through two significant Python

torchvision transforms. These tools collaborate to perform all the image
preprocessing tasks that are required, such as resizing images to the appropriate
dimensions, color corrections and brightness adjustments where necessary, and file
format changes for images where necessary.
The part of our system that users actually see and interact with, which developers
call the frontend, is constructed using the most current versions of standard web

Throughout the development and testing process, our team relied heavily on Jupiter

4. METHODOLOGY
Our project aimed to build An intelligent computing system capable of
automatically spot environmental problems like illegal tree cutting, garbage piles,
or polluted water directly from pictures. To make this system, we followed a clear
plan that involved four main steps: first, getting our image data ready; second,
choosing and setting up the "brain" for our system; third, teaching it what to look
for; and finally, carefully checking its performance.
First, we focused on preparing our data. We collected many photos and carefully
sorted them into three specific groups: "cutting trees," "garbage," and "polluted
water." To help our system learn efficiently, We categorized these images into two
groups : a "training" set, which was like the main textbook our system would study
from, and a "validation" set, which was used as a practice test to see how well it
was learning. To get our system more adaptive and robust, we employed a method
known as "data augmentation" on the training images. This involved applying
minor, different modifications to the figures, such as rotating them slightly, flipping
them, or changing their brightness.
After that, we proceeded with choosing and deploying our deep learning model,
which, is basically the "brain" of the smart system. We chose a strong kind of neural
network by the name of EfficientNet-B0. This model is unique because it had
already been "pretrained" on a more dataset of common images. That is, it has many
knowledge about recognizing different objects, so we didn't train again. This
technique is referred to as "transfer learning," and it not only saved us huge amounts
of time but also made the learning process easily more efficiently. We had a strong
foundation on which we placed some additional special layers on top. These
additional layers were specially designed to make the model concentrate especially
on discriminating between our three specific categories of environmental problems.
Once the model was established, the major step was to train it. In this process, the
model learned by repeatedly observing the preprocessed images of the training
dataset. We employed a specific mathematical equation called CrossEntropyLoss
to calculate how erroneous its predictions were; the larger the error, the more the
model had to learn and change. To assist it in learning well, we employed an
"optimizer" named AdamW and a "learning rate scheduler. Both of them exercised
utmost care to guide the model in making the proper adjustments to its inner
parameters. We presented the images to the model in batches (16 images at once)
repeatedly across many "epochs" (each epoch being a complete pass through all of
the training data, up to 30 times). We also employed "mixed precision training"
which aided in accelerating the learning process, particularly on high-end
computers. To avoid the model simply "memorizing" the group without actually
understanding (a condition referred to as "overfitting"), we employed an "early
stopping" criterion. If the model performance showed no improvement on the
validation set for 10 subsequent epochs, we would terminate the training. We
always retained the version of the model which performed best on the validation
set.
And lastly, we conducted a complete assessment to observe the effectiveness of our
trained model actually fared in real-world applications. Knowing if it was
"accurate" was not enough; we needed an in-depth view. We created a
"classification report" that provided us with concrete scores such as "precision" and
"recall" for every environmental problem, with an indication of how well the model
could identify each issue correctly and prevent making incorrect predictions. We
also built a "confusion matrix," a graphical table that simply indicates when the
model may get one environmental problem confused with another. That helped us
see if there was any particular place where the model may get stuck. We also
illustrated the capability of the model by making predictions on individual images
and projecting random data instances from our validation set to provide a clear
visual impression of its capability. This entire methodology made sure we were
able to create a solid and viable system for determining key environmental issues.

7.3.1 MACHINE LEARNING APPROACH
The machine learning approach Employs deep learning methods to classify images.
The system employs transfer learning with a pre-trained EfficientNet-B0 model as
the foundation. This approach takes advantage of knowledge learned from millions
of images to improve performance on environmental issue classification.
Transfer learning works by starting with a model already trained on general image
recognition tasks. The system then fine-tunes this model specifically for
environmental issue classification. This method needs fewer training examples and
delivers superior performance compared to building a model from the ground up.
The learning phase uses data augmentation techniques to increase the effective size
of the dataset. Random rotations, flips, and color adjustments create variations of
existing images that help the model learn more robust features. These techniques
prevent overfitting and improve model generalization.
The mathematical foundation relies on convolutional neural networks that process
images through multiple layers of feature extraction. Every layer progressively
captures more intricate patterns, starting with simple edges and progressing to
complete objects and scenes.

8. SOFTWARE TESTING
Testing represents one of the most critical phases in developing any software
application, and our environmental complaint management system required
comprehensive testing procedures to ensure it would function reliably when
deployed for public use. The value of through testing cannot be overemphasized
because it is our main way of detecting potential problems, bugs, and performance
problems prior to real users engaging with the system. By throwing accurate testing
or shortcutting it, we risk rolling out a system that will crash when people most
need it, lose critical complaint data, or fail to accurately classify environmental
concerns that citizens report. Our testing plan was intended to catch these issues
early on in the phase of development during which they are much less difficult and
costly to correct, instead of finding them after The system has been released for
public use.
We took a methodical test strategy that conformed to what software engineers refer
to as the testing pyramid approach, whereby we began by testing individual discrete
elements of our code and progressed upwards in scope to test increasing amounts
of larger, more complex portions of the system. At the unit level, we performed unit
testing in which we verified every single method and function in our code to ensure
they were executing their respective duties accurately. For instance, we tested if our
functions that resized images correctly minimized photo file sizes without
compromising key visual information, and if our database connection functions had
the ability to save and retrieve complaint data successfully. Having finished unit
testing, we shifted to integration testing, where we verified the interaction of
various components of our system. This involved testing if Our machine learning
system is capable of receive images from our upload system correctly and provide
the right classification results, as well as whether our user authentication went well
with our process of submitting complaints.
Our last layer of testing was extensive system testing, through which we tested the
entire environmental complaint management system as an integrated, working
application. Through this step, we simulated realistic circumstances that real users
would face, including citizens filing complaints with attached multiple photos,
administrators checking and correcting complaint statuses, and the system dealing
with scenarios under which internet connections can be slow and unreliable. We
also performed what is termed stress testing, wherein we purposely attempted to
overload our system through simulations of many users accessing it at the same
time, uploading large file sizes, and initiating several actions at the same time. This
stress testing allowed us to identify the potential bottlenecks and Ensure that our
system was able to sustain high-traffic times when lots of people would be reporting
environmental concerns during emergencies or community functions.
Performance testing was another key step in our testing protocol, where we gauged
how fast our system performed various types of requests and operations. We tested
the time it takes for our artificial intelligence model to scan and classify uploaded
photos, how quickly users can fill out the complaint filing process, and how
effectively administrators can browse through and process high volumes of
complaints. We also checked our system's reaction under several difficult
situations, including when users upload files in other formats than expected, when
individuals attempt to use features, they are not allowed to use, or when network
connections break while uploading files.
During our testing cycle, we kept extensive records of all the testing that we did,
noting what we wanted to occur in each situation and comparing it to what actually
occurred. This methodical documentation process allowed us to monitor what
features were functioning as they should and which had to be further developed or
fixed. We also developed test cases that could be reused again to make sure that
fixes we made did not inadvertently break other areas of the system. The testing
cycle took a lot of time and patience from our development team, but the investment
was well worth it as it paid off in a solid, dependable system that citizens and
administrators can rely upon for managing environmental complaints in a efficient
and effective manner.
8.1 TESTING METHODS
Unit Testing
It means checking individual pieces of code separately to make sure each part works
on its own. In our project, we tested every function separately such as the login
function, image upload function, and prediction function. We used testing tools
inbuilt in Python to design test scenarios to validate whether every function returns
the correct output when we provide it a certain input.
For instance, we tested the user registration functionality by passing it various kinds
of data such as good emails, bad emails, short passwords, and long passwords. We
also tested our AI prediction functionality by passing it sample images and
verifying if it identifies correctly garbage, tree cutting, or water pollution. We tested
each function to ensure that it was verifying by one particular thing at a time.
Unit testing assisted us in discovering diminutive defect in our code before they
turned into larger issues. When we detected a bug in a function, we were able to
immediately correct it without influencing other areas of the system. This facilitated
faster and more systematic development. We executed these tests repeatedly during
development to ensure new changes did not break old functionality.
Integration Testing
It verifies if various components of our system play nice with each other. Once we
ensured each function was working correctly, we must test how these connect and
communicate with one another. In our environmental system, we tested connections
among the website interface, database, AI model, and file storage system.
We exercised cases such as when a user submits a complaint form, the application
should store the text to the database, save images to the file system, execute the AI
prediction, and then display a success message. All of these The components need
to work together smoothly. We exercised the link between user login and the
process of submission of complaints as well.
Another key integration test was whether the admin dashboard properly fetches
data from the database and displays it accordingly. We ensured that whenever an
admin modifies a complaint status, it reflects in the database as well as informs both
admin and normal users about it. Integration testing aided us in detecting issues that
exist only when various components of the system attempt to work together
seamlessly, which unit testing failed to identify.
System Testing
This refers to testing the entire application like actual users are working on it. We
thoroughly tested the entire environmental complaint system from beginning to
end, including all the features and functionalities. This type of testing ensures that
the system meets all requirements defined at the beginning of the project.
We tested the entire user path from account registration, log-in, submitting
complaints with images, reading complaint status, and log-out. For admins, we
tested the entire process of log-in, seeing all complaints, status change, adding
notes, and report generation. We also tested the behavior of the system when used
by many at the same time.
System testing also involved verifying the accuracy of the AI model using actual
images of the environment, running file upload limits, verifying security aspects,
and ensuring website functionality with various web browsers and devices. We
tested error scenarios as well, such as how the website responds when the internet
is slow or when an attempt is made to upload excessively large files. All this
extensive testing left us assured that approach is ready for actual users in actual
environments.

This environmental complaint management system represents a significant step
forward in how we handle environmental issues in our communities. By integrating
web technology with artificial intelligence, we have developed a platform that
simplifies the task of reporting issues such as illegal felling of trees, dumping of
trash, and water pollution.
The machine learning model that we created had a stellar 91.08% accuracy in
automatically recognizing various environmental issues from uploaded
photographs. This high accuracy implies that the system can sort complaints with
confidence and allow administrators to address the most critical issues first. The
model functions well specifically with cutting trees (98.15% precision) and trash
issues (100% recall), indicating it can effectively deal with the most prevalent
environmental complaints.
The web app offers an easy-to-use experience whereby anyone can easily report
environmental issues by uploading images and completing a short form. Although
the system is functioning properly, there is always room for improvement.
Including support for additional environmental issue types, adding mobile
notifications, and developing enhanced data visualization tools could make the
platform more powerful. In general, this project shows how technology can be
utilized to help guard our environment and faster tighter relationships between
communities and environmental agencies.
